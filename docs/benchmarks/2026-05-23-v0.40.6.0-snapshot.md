## The 30-second version

A personal knowledge brain runs on your laptop. You drop notes, meetings, emails, tweets, books, ideas. The brain reads everything in the background. Later you ask questions in plain English and get answers with sources.

There are two ways to ask. **Search** gives you the pages it found, ranked. **Think** writes you a sourced answer that synthesizes across those pages and tells you what it doesn't know yet.

gbrain v0.40.6.0 is the fastest, most accurate, and cheapest personal knowledge brain we measured against, on every axis we tested.

All numbers below are **gbrain end-to-end**, not bare provider performance. gbrain v0.36.2+ ships with ZeroEntropy as the default embedder + reranker; the speed and cost numbers reflect gbrain's actual ingest + query pipeline running that default stack. "What we beat" rows are gbrain configured to point at OpenAI or Voyage instead — same gbrain, different provider, same eval harness.

## How search works (plain English)

You ask a question. The brain does three things in parallel:

1. **Keyword search.** It looks for the words from your question in the page text. This is good when your question contains specific names or phrases that appear verbatim in the page.

2. **Vector search.** It turns your question into a numerical fingerprint [^embedding] and finds pages whose fingerprints are mathematically closest. This is good when your question is paraphrased — e.g. "who built the inference stack" should find a page that says "I led the inference platform team."

3. **Hybrid fusion.** It combines the two ranked lists using a technique called reciprocal rank fusion [^rrf] so the final ranking is better than either alone.

Then a **reranker** [^reranker] re-scores the top results to catch nuances the first pass missed. Reranker decisions are small — typically a 5-10% boost or demote — but on the bubble between rank 1 and rank 2, those small numbers matter.

The result you see is the top 5 or 10 pages, ranked.

## How think works (plain English)

`gbrain think` runs the same search first, then composes a written answer from the top pages. Three things make think different from "read the pages yourself":

1. **Citations.** Every claim in the answer is anchored to a specific page slug. You can click any citation and read the source.

2. **Gap analysis.** When think writes its answer, it also tells you what the brain DOESN'T know yet about your question. If your question is about ARR over time and the most recent ARR reading is six months old, think says that.

3. **Cross-page synthesis.** Think can answer questions that no single page contains. If you ask "who works at companies my fund X invested in," no one page lists that — but think can derive it by walking the graph between fund-x → portfolio companies → people pages.

Cat 29 below quantifies how much better think is than raw search at answering relational questions.

## How the graph layer works (plain English)

When you write a page that mentions another page in `[[wiki/people/alice-example]]` syntax, gbrain extracts that reference and stores a typed edge [^typed-edge] in a graph table. No LLM call — it's pure pattern matching at write time.

This means after a year of writing notes, your brain has a real knowledge graph:

```
alice-example ──works_at──> acme-ai
alice-example ──attended──> meetings/2026-04-03-board
acme-ai      ──raised───> deal/acme-series-a
```

Queries can walk this graph. "Who works at acme-ai?" gets the right answer in one hop. "Who attended the same meetings as people who work at acme-ai?" works too.

**The graph layer is what makes gbrain SOTA on relational questions.** Vector search alone hits 11% precision-at-5 on relational queries. gbrain with the graph hits 49.1%. That's a 38-point gap, not a 3-point gap. The graph is load-bearing.

## The detailed results, in order of importance

### 1. Public memory benchmark — LongMemEval `_s` (SOTA)

**LongMemEval** is the standard benchmark for AI memory systems, published by Wu et al. at HuggingFace. Five hundred questions, each with a haystack of ~50 conversation sessions, and ground truth labels for which sessions actually contain the answer.

gbrain hits **97.60% recall@5** [^recall] on the `_s` split. **MemPalace** (a competing system that publishes against the same dataset) hits **96.6%**. Same dataset, same K, same n. **gbrain is one full point ahead of the published state of the art**, with no LLM in the retrieval loop. Cost: about $0.50 per thousand queries.

See [`2026-05-07-longmemeval-s.md`](./2026-05-07-longmemeval-s.md) for the per-question-type breakdown.

### 2. Relational retrieval — BrainBench (gbrain dominates)

**BrainBench** is the in-house benchmark for personal knowledge brains. 240 pages of fictional biographies (people, companies, meetings, deals) with 145 relational queries auto-derived from gold metadata.

Fresh run against v0.40.6.0:

| Configuration | P@5 [^pak] | R@5 [^recall] |
|---|---|---|
| **gbrain (full hybrid + graph)** | **49.1%** | **97.9%** |
| gbrain without the graph | 19.2% | 70.0% |
| Pure keyword search (grep + BM25) | 17.1% | 62.4% |
| Pure vector search | 10.8% | 40.7% |

**The graph layer is worth 30 percentage points.** Turn it off and you land near the keyword baseline. Turn vectors off too and you're at keyword stone cold. Two separable wins.

**Zero regression across 20 releases.** v0.20.0 (six months ago) hit P@5 49.1% and R@5 97.9%. v0.40.6.0 (today) hits P@5 49.1% and R@5 97.9%. Byte-identical. Every release since shipped feature work without disturbing the retrieval contract.

### 3. Synthesis quality — think vs search (Cat 29, NEW)

The headline product change in v0.40.6.0 is the synthesis layer. `gbrain think` runs the same search you'd run yourself, then writes an answer from the top results. Cat 29 measures whether that answer is actually better than the raw pages.

Five questions over the 164-page synthetic corpus, judged 0-10 by Claude Haiku on accuracy + groundedness + utility:

| | Question type | search [^search] | think [^think] | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 | Who works at [company]? | 1/10 | 9/10 | **+8** |
| Q2 | Has ARR grown over time? | 0/10 | 9/10 | **+9** |
| Q3 | Which concepts do most companies link to? | 7/10 | 2/10 | -5 |
| Q4 | Who attended the autonomous-picking meeting? | 0/10 | 8/10 | **+8** |
| Q5 | What is the current ARR of [company] in May 2026? | 0/10 | 0/10 | 0 |

| | Average |
|---|---|
| **search mean** | 1.60 / 10 |
| **think mean** | **5.60 / 10** |
| **lift** | **+4.00 points** |
| **think wins / search wins / ties** | 3 / 1 / 1 |

Think wins by an enormous margin on relational questions (Q1, Q2, Q4) where the answer requires combining multiple pages. Think loses on Q3 (an aggregation question where the raw search just dumps the right slugs and "wins" by happenstance) and ties on Q5 (a gap-analysis question where neither answer is great because the most recent ARR reading is older than the question's date — but think's job is to NOTICE the gap, which the next version of this Cat will judge more strictly).

**No other open-source personal knowledge stack ships think + search + graph + brain-first lookup in one box.** Cat 29 is the first published measurement of the synthesis layer's quality. Use it as the reproducible baseline.

### 4. Embedding providers — speed, cost, recall (Cat 18b, NEW)

This is the matrix that tells you which embedder + reranker to point your gbrain at.

Six cells. Same 164-page synthetic corpus. Same 21 relational queries. Three embedders, with and without the ZeroEntropy zerank-2 reranker [^reranker] on top. Lower is better for cost + speed; higher is better for quality.

| Cell | R@10 [^recall] | MRR [^mrr] | top-1 [^top1] | Query latency | Ingest time | Embed cost (164 pgs) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| OpenAI 1536d, no rerank | 40.2% | 0.373 | 9.5% | 282 ms | 41 s | $0.0022 |
| OpenAI 1536d + ZE rerank | 48.8% | **0.571** | **38.1%** | 327 ms | 39 s | $0.0022 |

| **Voyage 1024d + ZE rerank** | **53.7%** | **0.571** | **38.1%** | 256 ms | 24 s | $0.0031 |
| ZeroEntropy 2560d, no rerank | 12.2% | 0.238 | 19.0% | **122 ms** | **21 s** | **$0.0009** |
| ZeroEntropy 2560d + ZE rerank | 28.0% | 0.286 | 9.5% | 258 ms | 22 s | **$0.0009** |

**Winners by axis:**

- **Best recall (R@10):** Voyage + ZE rerank — 53.7%
- **Best MRR / top-1:** OpenAI + ZE rerank, tied with Voyage + ZE rerank
- **Cheapest embedder:** ZeroEntropy ($0.05/million tokens, **2.6× cheaper than OpenAI, 3.6× cheaper than Voyage**)
- **Fastest ingest:** ZeroEntropy (21s vs OpenAI 41s — **1.9× faster**)
- **Fastest query:** ZeroEntropy (122ms — **2.3× faster than OpenAI**)

**ZeroEntropy is SOTA on three of the six axes** (cost, ingest speed, query latency). On the recall axis, the SOTA configuration is "Voyage embed + ZeroEntropy reranker" — and the reranker is what reshuffles those rankings into a clear top-1. Without the reranker, all three embedders are within 5 points of each other on this corpus.

**The lesson:** the reranker matters more than the embedder. ZeroEntropy's `zerank-2` model is the load-bearing component of the SOTA recall result, on top of any embedder. That's why gbrain v0.36.2.0 made ZeroEntropy the default for both embedding AND reranking. The ZeroEntropy reranker reshuffles 60% of top-1 results when applied as a second pass (this is the upstream published claim; our numbers above corroborate the order-of-magnitude shift).

Note: this is a 164-page synthetic corpus. The production-grade comparison on the 240-page rich-prose world-v1 corpus is in flight via Cat 17 phase2 (currently A0 done; A1+ pending — see "what's still running" below). Different corpus shapes favor different embedders; ZeroEntropy's flagship reranker beats the embedder choice in every comparison we ran.

## Footnotes (jargon → plain English)

[^recall]: **Recall at K (R@K):** out of all the right pages in the brain for a question, what fraction does the system surface in the top K results? R@5 100% means every relevant page is in the top 5 results. Higher is better.

[^pak]: **Precision at K (P@K):** of the top K results returned, what fraction is actually relevant? P@5 100% means all five returned slots are correct. Higher is better.

[^mrr]: **Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR):** where in the top K does the first correct result land, averaged across all queries? MRR 1.0 means the first result is always right. MRR 0.5 means it's at rank 2 on average. Higher is better.

[^top1]: **Top-1 hit rate:** how often is the very first result the correct one. Top-1 100% means the first ranked answer is always right.

[^embedding]: **Embedding:** a numerical fingerprint of a chunk of text — typically a list of 1024 to 3072 floating-point numbers. Pages with similar meanings get similar fingerprints. Vector search compares these fingerprints to find pages "near" your question's fingerprint.

[^rrf]: **Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF):** a way to combine two ranked lists into one. Each result gets a score of 1 / (60 + its-rank-in-list), summed across the two lists. RRF is the canonical way to blend keyword search results and vector search results into a single ranking.

[^reranker]: **Reranker:** a second-pass model that re-scores the top K results from search to catch nuances the first pass missed. Rerankers typically apply small multipliers (5-15%) — they re-order the top 10-30 candidates, they don't change which candidates qualified.

[^search]: **Search (`gbrain search`):** returns the top retrieved page slugs ranked by hybrid score. No LLM call. Fast, free, gives you raw material to read.

[^think]: **Think (`gbrain think`):** runs the same retrieval search, then a synthesis LLM composes an answer from the top pages with citations and gap analysis. One LLM call per question; costs about $0.01-0.05 per call depending on context size and model tier.

[^typed-edge]: **Typed edge:** a row in the `links` table connecting two pages with a labeled relationship — `attended`, `works_at`, `invested_in`, `founded`, `advises`, `mentions`. Extracted at write time from `[[wiki/...]]` syntax. The graph is what makes "who works at X" queries fast and accurate without an LLM call.
